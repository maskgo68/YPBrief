**# Podcast Name**  
Invest Like The Best

**# Video Title**  
Legendary Trader Paul Tudor Jones on AI Risk, Bubbles and Buffett

**# Publish Date**  
2026-04-28

**# Core Summary**  
Paul Tudor Jones reflects on 50 years as a macro trader, contrasting the relentless, high-frequency risk management required in trading with the long-term compounding mindset of investors like Warren Buffett, whom he now calls the “OG of compound interest” after previously criticizing him. He expresses acute alarm over the unregulated development of AI, arguing that its “build-break-iterate” model carries unprecedented tail risks that could cost hundreds of millions of lives, with no public oversight or regulatory framework three years after major model releases. The central takeaway is that exceptional risk management, clarity of thought, and intentional daily acts of kindness are non-negotiable for both sustained success and a meaningful life.

**# Key Points**  
- Jones’s childhood experience of being lost at age 2–3 and helped by an elderly Black man led to 10–12 years of nightly prayers for that stranger; this single act of kindness directly inspired him to create the Robin Hood Foundation after the 1987 crash and later found the Bed-Stuy Charter School of Excellence, which ranked #1 out of 543 NYC elementary schools within five years. He emphasizes that one small act can create “waves of betterment” and advocates starting every day with the deliberate goal of performing one.

- After five decades of trading, Jones admits he was wrong about Buffett. His own BVI fund has maintained a –0.12 correlation to the S&P 500 over 40 years, generating 100% alpha, yet he envies Buffett’s calm belief in America during 50% drawdowns. He credits Buffett with understanding the power of compound interest at age nine and forming the perfect partnership with Charlie Munger, who focused on high-quality compounders.

- AI is the most serious risk Jones has encountered in his career. Current deployment follows a “build-break-iterate” model where the “break” could kill hundreds of millions or billions of people. At a private conference with modelers from the four largest labs, the consensus was that meaningful safety measures would only arrive after 50–100 million deaths. Unlike the nuclear bomb, which prompted the Atomic Energy Commission within 18 months, AI has seen almost no regulatory response after three years. Jones calls for immediate presidential leadership to convene global regulation, especially with China.

- The single most practical policy step is to require all AI output to be watermarked, with repeated knowing violations treated as a felony. This would restore truth, combat deepfakes (which have already fooled Jones twice this year), and allow society to distinguish authentic human content from machine-generated material before brain-computer interfaces become widespread.

- Current U.S. equity valuations are extreme: stock market capitalization stands at 252% of GDP, compared with 65% in 1929, ~90% in 1987, and 170% in 2000. Jones notes a reversal in equity supply dynamics—buybacks have retired 2–3% of market cap annually for a decade, but forthcoming IPOs and unlocks could add 5–6% of market cap over the next year while hyperscaler capex reduces corporate cash available for repurchases. A mean-reversion to more normal valuations would imply a 30–35% decline and significant negative wealth effects given that 10% of federal tax revenue comes from capital gains.

- Trading, for Jones, is identifying prolonged imbalances, waiting for a catalytic event, and executing with precision at points of maximum fear or greed. Examples include shorting two-year Treasuries in 2022 after Powell stayed too easy to secure reappointment, buying Bitcoin in 2020 as the superior finite inflation hedge, and the emerging dollar-yen opportunity created by Japan’s new “Japan-first” prime minister who resembles Reagan or Thatcher. He learned from mentor Eli Tullis the importance of maintaining composure and confidence even after catastrophic losses (e.g., a limit-down cotton position after a weekend rain).

**# Why It Matters**  
Jones delivers one of the clearest articulations of AI’s asymmetric tail risks from a professional risk manager who has navigated every major crisis since the 1970s. His call for immediate watermarking and global coordination highlights a dangerous regulatory vacuum with direct implications for national security, labor markets, and social trust. For investors and allocators, the valuation and equity-supply analysis provides a concrete framework for stress-testing portfolios against a potential 30–35% mean-reversion event and the reversal of a decade-long buyback tailwind. Beyond markets, his emphasis on disciplined thinking (via journalism training), composure under pressure, and the multiplicative power of simple kindness offers practical guidance for both professional excellence and long-term personal significance in an increasingly polarized and AI-disrupted world.
